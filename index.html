KEY TERMS & GLOSSARY
DOCG
Italy's highest wine classification, guaranteeing both origin and strict production standards.
NV (Non-Vintage)
Wine blended from multiple harvest years rather than a single vintage.
Charmat Method
Secondary fermentation in a pressurized tank — the method used for Prosecco, faster and more economical than bottle fermentation.
Méthode Champenoise
Secondary fermentation inside the individual bottle, creating Champagne's fine, persistent bubbles.
Skin Maceration
Extended contact between juice and grape skins to extract color and flavor — how Laurent-Perrier makes its rosé rather than blending in red wine.
Cuvée
French term for a specific blend or batch of wine; Grand Siècle is Laurent-Perrier's prestige multi-vintage cuvée.
Dosage
A small amount of wine and sugar added to Champagne before final corking to control the finished sweetness level.
Vacuum Distillation
Low-temperature alcohol removal that preserves aromatic compounds — how Wolfer Estate produces its non-alcoholic wine.
Provençal Rosé Style
Always dry, pale salmon, with only 12–24 hours of skin contact — mineral, fresh, and food-forward.
KEY CONCEPTS
1

Adami's Bosco di Gica is a single-vineyard Prosecco DOCG from 40-year-old vines on the Valdobbiadene hillsides — a terroir-specific approach uncommon in a region dominated by large-scale volume production.

2

Cleto Chiarli is the oldest Lambrusco producer in Modena, founded in 1860 — their version is completely dry and food-forward, the opposite of the sweet Lambrusco that flooded the American market in the 1970s.

3

Laurent-Perrier pioneered rosé Champagne in 1968 using skin maceration of Pinot Noir only — most rosé Champagne is made by blending a small amount of red still wine in; maceration is rarer and produces a wine of distinctive purity.

4

Grand Siècle blends multiple exceptional vintages rather than declaring a single year — Bernard de Nonancourt's philosophy was that consistency across great harvests outperforms any single year's limitations.

5

Triennes Rosé was co-founded by Aubert de Villaine of Domaine de la Romanée-Conti; Provence rosé is always dry with pale color from just 12-24 hours of skin contact — the opposite of sweet pink wines many guests assume.

6

Wolfer Estate uses vacuum distillation to preserve aromatics without heat — it functions as a genuine wine experience at the table and is the most important tool for including non-drinking guests in a wine pairing moment.

KEY TERMS & GLOSSARY
Appellation / AVA
A legally defined and protected wine-growing region; AVA stands for American Viticultural Area.
Négociant
A wine merchant who sources grapes from growers and sells under their own label.
Biodynamic Farming
A holistic farming approach treating the vineyard as a self-sustaining ecosystem — more rigorous than certified organic.
Kimmeridgian Chalk
Ancient seabed limestone soil found in Sancerre that gives the wine its distinctive flinty, saline mineral character.
Terroir
All environmental factors affecting a wine's character — soil, climate, elevation, and sun exposure.
Pyrazines
Chemical compounds responsible for grassy, herbaceous aromas in some Sauvignon Blancs; more prominent in New Zealand than in Loire Valley.
Estate Wine
Wine made exclusively from grapes grown on the winery's own property.
Flinty / Saline
Mineral descriptors common in chalk-soil wines — flinty suggests struck stone, saline suggests a salt-like freshness.

KEY TERMS & GLOSSARY
Malolactic Fermentation (MLF)
Converts sharp malic acid to softer lactic acid, creating the buttery, creamy texture found in wines like The Prisoner.
Oak Aging
Aging wine in wooden barrels, adding vanilla, toast, and creamy notes — new oak barrels have stronger impact than older ones.
Cool-Climate Wine
Wine from regions with lower average temperatures, producing higher natural acidity and more restrained fruit.
Pouilly-Fuissé
A Burgundy appellation in the Mâconnais known for mineral, food-driven Chardonnay — Domaine Ferret's home.
Lees / Sur Lie
Spent yeast cells left after fermentation; aging on them adds richness and a creamy texture common in Burgundy Chardonnay.
Small-Production Winery
Makes very limited quantities of wine, contributing to scarcity and higher price — as with DuMOL.

Italy's classification below DOC/DOCG, used for Super Tuscans that fall outside traditional regional rules.
DOCG
Italy's highest wine quality classification — both Brunello di Montalcino and Amarone della Valpolicella carry this designation.
Super Tuscan
Tuscan wines using non-traditional grapes outside DOC rules, pioneered by Antinori in the 1970s.
Corvina
The primary grape of Valpolicella and Amarone — when dried through appassimento, it produces extraordinary richness and complexity.
KEY CONCEPTS
1

Super Tuscans are IGT-labeled wines using non-traditional Italian grapes (Cabernet Sauvignon, Merlot, Syrah) — the designation was created partly because wines like Sassicaia were too good to be labeled table wine but did not fit DOC rules.

2

Brunello di Montalcino is 100% Sangiovese from a specific hillside zone — Riserva versions require a minimum of 6 years aging before release, and Antinori's Pian delle Vigne is one of the estate benchmarks in this appellation.

3

Q2 of 5
A guest asks what makes Amarone different from every other Italian red they have ever tried.
Amarone is the only Italian red wine permitted to blend grapes from multiple appellations.
It is made from Nebbiolo grapes grown at high altitude in the Veneto Dolomites.
Amarone ferments twice as long as other Italian reds, which creates the distinctive style.
The grapes are dried for months before fermentation, concentrating flavor and richness.
Amarone's defining characteristic is appassimento — Corvina and other Venetian grapes are laid on racks for 90-120 days, losing 30-40% of their water and concentrating sugars, color, and flavors before pressing and dry fermentation. The result is a wine of extraordinary richness, typically 15-16% alcohol. Amarone is not multi-appellation, not Nebbiolo-based, and fermentation length is not the key distinction.
Q3 of 5
What is a Super Tuscan and why does that category exist?
It is an IGT wine using non-traditional grapes — created because DOC rules were too restrictive.
Super Tuscans are Sangiovese wines from Chianti Classico at the very highest quality tier.
The term describes any Tuscan wine that earns a 95-plus point critical score in a vintage.
It is a legal DOC designation Italy created in 1992 to recognize Tuscany's finest estates.
The Super Tuscan category emerged in the 1970s when producers like Antinori started using Cabernet Sauvignon, Merlot, and Syrah in Tuscany — grapes outside traditional DOC rules. These wines were labeled as humble table wine despite their quality. Italy eventually created the IGT designation partly in response. It is not about Sangiovese excellence, critical scores, or a new DOC classification.

How would you explain Borgogno's No Name Nebbiolo to a guest who has never encountered it?
No Name is Borgogno's entry-level wine, made from young vines that cannot qualify for Barolo.
The no name refers to the fact that Nebbiolo has no accepted international name or synonym.
It is Piedmont Nebbiolo with no marketing — a statement about letting the grape speak.
Borgogno releases it without a name because they change the blend significantly each vintage.
Borgogno's No Name is a deliberate philosophical choice — one of Piedmont's oldest estates (founded 1761) stripping away label prestige and marketing narrative to focus attention on Nebbiolo as a grape and Piedmont as a place. It is not an entry-level wine based on vine age restrictions, it is not about blend inconsistency, and it is not about international name recognition. The name is a statement about identity.
