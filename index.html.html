WINEMAKER & ESTATE STORIES

Adami has farmed the Valdobbiadene hillsides since 1920 — four generations deep in the Prosecco heartland. Their Bosco di Gica is a single-vineyard wine from vines over 40 years old, rare in a region dominated by volume production. Cleto Chiarli was founded in 1860 in Modena and is the oldest Lambrusco producer in the region — they essentially defined the style the world came to know. Laurent-Perrier was founded in 1812 and remained family-controlled for generations under the de Nonancourt family. Their Cuvée Rosé, introduced in 1968, pioneered the rosé Champagne category and is still made by macerating only Pinot Noir skins — no blending of red wine, which sets it apart technically and philosophically. Grand Siècle is their prestige multi-vintage blend; Bernard de Nonancourt called it 'recreating the perfect vintage.'

1

Adami's Bosco di Gica is a single-vineyard Prosecco DOCG from 40-year-old vines on the Valdobbiadene hillsides — a rare, terroir-specific approach in a region known for large-scale industrial production.

2

Laurent-Perrier pioneered rosé Champagne in 1968 using skin maceration of Pinot Noir only — no blending red wine in, which is the standard allowed method; this makes the Cuvée Rosé technically and historically distinctive.

3

Grand Siècle blends multiple exceptional vintages rather than declaring a single year — Bernard de Nonancourt's philosophy was that consistency across great harvests outperforms any single year's limitations.

4

Cleto Chiarli's Lambrusco is dry and food-forward — the opposite of the sweet Lambrusco imported to the US in the 1970s; it's a sparkling red with real tannin and savory edge.

5

Wolfer Estate's non-alcoholic rosé uses vacuum distillation to remove alcohol at low temperature, preserving aromatic compounds that heat-based methods would destroy — it's a technically serious wine, not a substitute.

Q2 of 5
What technically distinguishes Laurent-Perrier Cuvée Rosé from most other rosé Champagnes?
It's made by skin maceration of Pinot Noir — no red wine blended in.
It blends red Burgundy wine into the base Chardonnay for pink color.
It uses a longer second bottle fermentation than competing houses.
It sources exclusively from Grand Cru Pinot Noir villages in the Marne.
Laurent-Perrier's Cuvée Rosé uses skin maceration — Pinot Noir skins are in contact with the juice long enough to extract color and character before pressing. Most rosé Champagne is made by blending a small amount of red still wine into the base; maceration is rarer, more labor-intensive, and gives the wine a distinctive depth and purity. The other options describe either inaccurate methods or details that don't define the wine's distinction.
Q3 of 5
What is the core production difference between Prosecco and Champagne?
Prosecco ferments in tanks; Champagne ferments in the individual bottle.
Prosecco skips secondary fermentation; Champagne always has two full fermentations.
Champagne uses only Chardonnay; Prosecco is made from multiple Italian grapes.
Prosecco must be vintage-dated; Champagne is almost always non-vintage blended.
Prosecco's second fermentation happens in large pressurized tanks (Charmat method) — faster and more economical. Champagne's second fermentation happens inside the individual bottle (méthode champenoise), creating finer, more persistent bubbles and greater yeast-derived complexity. Both methods produce sparkling wine, but the secondary fermentation location defines the style. Champagne uses multiple grape varieties, and both wines have vintage and non-vintage expressions.
Q4 of 5

Present Wolfer Estate non-alcoholic rosé with genuine enthusiasm and context.

Offer the Lambrusco since it has lower alcohol than Champagne by comparison.
Redirect them to the bar team to explore mocktail options instead.
Wolfer Estate is made by vacuum distillation specifically to function as a real wine experience at the table — especially meaningful for a birthday celebration. Presenting it with genuine context ('made the same way as wine, just without the alcohol — it's actually really lovely') honors the guest. Sparkling water is a missed opportunity. Lambrusco still contains alcohol. Redirecting to mocktails takes them out of the wine program entirely.
Q5 of 5
A guest asks to 'just bring the best sparkling wine' on the list. How do you respond?
Ask what they're eating — the best sparkling depends on the context and occasion.
Bring the Grand Siècle immediately — it's the highest price and therefore the answer.
Offer the Prosecco since it's the most versatile and crowd-pleasing option available.
Explain that all sparklings are equally excellent and let them decide on their own.
The honest professional answer is that 'best' depends on context: Grand Siècle is the most complex and prestigious, but it may be over-specified for light starters or the wrong wine for a guest who prefers fresh and simple. A quick question — 'Are you celebrating something? What are you thinking of ordering?' — gives you the information to make a genuinely right recommendation. Defaulting to highest price without context isn't expertise.

WINEMAKER & ESTATE STORIES

CADE Estate sits on Howell Mountain in Napa at 1,800 feet — owned by PlumpJack Group, the same family behind PlumpJack Winery and a network of Bay Area hospitality. Mountain-grown Sauvignon Blanc is rare in Napa; volcanic soils and elevation give it a minerality that valley-floor versions can't replicate. Illumination is the white wine from Quintessa, Napa's renowned single-estate red blend. Quintessa was founded by Agustin and Valeria Huneeus in 1990 on 280 acres in Rutherford — biodynamically farmed, every acre managed as a single organism. Illumination is the white expression of that philosophy. Knights Bridge is a 35-acre Sonoma estate focused almost entirely on single-vineyard whites — a boutique scale that's rare in California. Sager & Verdier is a Loire négociant producing Sancerre from Kimmeridgian chalk — the same ancient seabed soils that define great Chablis — giving the wine its signature flinty, saline mineral edge.

1

CADE's Howell Mountain location at 1,800 feet means cooler growing conditions despite Napa's warm reputation — volcanic soils and elevation produce Sauvignon Blanc with tension, brightness, and minerality uncommon on the valley floor.

2

Illumination carries the prestige of the Quintessa estate name — guests who know the Quintessa red (\$275) immediately understand why this white is priced at \$105, and it's biodynamically farmed to the same standard.

3

Sancerre's Kimmeridgian chalk gives the wine a flinty, almost saline quality — this mineral expression is the defining characteristic that separates Loire Sauvignon Blanc from the herbaceous New Zealand style or the richer California version.

4

Knights Bridge at \$102 is a boutique single-vineyard Sonoma expression — the value story is about scale and attention, not pedigree, and it sits in a different conversation than Illumination or Sancerre.

5

For food pairing at Eating House, Sauvignon Blanc is the strongest white for the Yellowfin Tuna Tiradito (passionfruit, sweet corn, lime) — the citrus bridge between wine and dish is direct and compelling.

At 1,800 feet on Howell Mountain, CADE benefits from cooler temperatures than Napa's valley floor, plus volcanic soils that add mineral complexity. This combination creates Sauvignon Blanc with more tension, brightness, and structure than warmer valley sites. It's not an AVA restriction on grape varieties, it's not primarily a color story, and mountain vineyards don't inherently produce higher alcohol — often the opposite.

The honest answer builds trust: they're genuinely different wines. Sancerre's Kimmeridgian chalk produces lean, flinty, saline Sauvignon Blanc — ideal for delicate seafood. Framing it that way is more credible than declaring a winner.

WINEMAKER & ESTATE STORIES

Morgan Winery was founded in 1982 by Dan and Donna Lee in Carmel Valley — they were among the first to recognize Santa Lucia Highlands as a serious Chardonnay region, where cold Pacific fog and high elevation produce natural acidity rare in California. The Prisoner Wine Company was created by Dave Phinney in 2000 and sold for \$285 million in 2010 after becoming one of California's most recognizable labels; their Chardonnay follows the same philosophy as their red — rich, generous, and broadly appealing. DuMOL was founded in 1996 by two Australian partners, Andy Smith and Roy CaBilan, as a small-production Sonoma project; 'Chloe' is named for a founder's daughter and has become one of California's most celebrated cool-climate Chardonnays. Domaine Ferret in Pouilly-Fuissé was run for decades by Colette Ferret-Lorton — a legendary Burgundy figure who produced wines of unusual depth before Louis Jadot acquired the estate in 2008 while preserving its identity and approach.

1

Morgan's Santa Lucia Highlands location at high elevation with Pacific fog influence produces Chardonnay with natural acidity that's uncommon in California — it's a genuinely cool-climate wine at a by-the-glass price point.

2

Dave Phinney built The Prisoner into a \$285M brand on the principle of rich, approachable, crowd-pleasing wine — their Chardonnay is intentionally riper and creamier, exactly right for guests who say they like 'buttery Chardonnay.'

3

DuMOL 'Chloe' from Russian River Valley is small-production, critically acclaimed, and one of California's benchmark cool-climate Chardonnays — the price reflects scarcity and quality, not marketing.

4

Domaine Ferret Pouilly-Fuissé represents the Burgundian tradition: mineral-driven, restrained oak, food-first — Colette Ferret-Lorton's philosophy of letting Chardonnay speak for its limestone terroir rather than winemaking intervention.

5

For Eating House's menu, Chardonnay bridges the widest range of dishes: Branzino Meuniere (citrus butter sauce mirrors the wine's texture), Chilean Seabass, and even Pasta Carbonara (richness meeting richness).

WINEMAKER & ESTATE STORIES

Masi is a Veneto institution founded in 1772, still family-owned by the Boscainis — their Masianco blends Pinot Grigio with Verduzzo for a texture and body unusual in the category. Bruno Verdi is a small estate in Oltrepò Pavese applying boutique winemaking attention to Pinot Grigio, a grape almost never treated this seriously; their \$105 bottle is a genuine argument that the grape deserves more respect. Trimbach has been family-run since 1626 — one of the oldest continuously operating wine estates in France — and their Riesling philosophy is bone-dry, built to age, and fiercely anti-sweet. Paco y Lola is a modern Galician cooperative producing Albariño from Atlantic granite soils in Rías Baixas — the saline, peach-forward freshness that defines the appellation. Emilio Moro is primarily known for Ribera del Duero reds, but their Godello from Bierzo represents a grape that was nearly extinct by the 1970s and has been dramatically revived by a small group of committed producers.

1

Pinot Grigio and Pinot Gris are the same grape — Italian Pinot Grigio is light and neutral; Alsatian Pinot Gris is rich and spicy; Bruno Verdi's version sits between these poles with unusual depth for the category.

2

Trimbach Dry Riesling from Alsace contains zero residual sugar — Trimbach has been making bone-dry Riesling since 1626 and it's one of their founding principles; the grape's natural acidity does all the work.

3

Paco y Lola Albariño from Rías Baixas is shaped by Atlantic influence: saline, peach-forward, high acid, medium body — ideal for seafood, particularly the Chilean Seabass on the Eating House menu.

4

Godello nearly disappeared in northwest Spain by the 1970s as growers pulled it out for higher-yielding varieties; its revival in Bierzo and Valdeorras is one of the great rescue stories in contemporary European wine.

5

The value tier here — Masi, Paco y Lola, Trimbach by the glass — is among the strongest on the entire wine list; these are genuinely excellent wines, not filler options.

Amarone's comparison to Pomerol overstates the similarity. Nebbiolo's tannin structure is genuine but very different from Cabernet.
